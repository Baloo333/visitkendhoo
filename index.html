An island that still remembers

Most visitors to the Maldives never set foot on an inhabited island. Kendhoo is one of thirteen lived-in islands in Baa Atoll — a working community of around a thousand people, where the harbour fills with dhonis at dusk, thatch is still woven by hand, and elders still point to the very spot where, local tradition holds, Islamic prayer was first offered in the Maldives. It doesn't perform its history for tourists. It simply still has one, in daily use.

🥥 Fani Kendhoo · "sweet Kendhoo"
menu_book THE NAMING LEGEND

The island that named itself "sweet"

Maldivian tradition holds that an Arab scholar — remembered here as Thabreyzgefaanu — who is widely credited with introducing Islam to the Maldives in 1153, stopped on this island and asked for a young coconut to drink. He found it so sweet that he renamed the island Fani Kendhoo, "sweet Kendhoo." The spot where he prayed was later marked by a coral-stone house, the Magaamfulhu, which still stands today.

celebration A WORKING ISLAND

Festivals, fishing & Friday prayers

Daily life here still runs on tide, prayer time and harvest. Eid is marked with Boduberu drumming and shared feasts, children's days fill the schoolyard, and the harbour empties and fills again with the fishing fleet. Visiting Kendhoo means stepping into a Maldivian island as residents actually live it — modest dress on the village streets, warm hospitality, and a pace set by the sea.
