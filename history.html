History & Heritage

A coral-stone shrine, a sweetened coconut, and the island said to have welcomed the man who brought Islam to the Maldives.

🥥 The legend of Fani Kendhoo

The scholar who called the coconut sweet

Long before resorts and seaplanes, Maldivian tradition holds that Kendhoo received a visitor whose stay would define the island's identity. An Arab scholar named Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari — remembered across the Maldives as Thabreyzgefaanu — is widely credited with persuading the Maldivian court to accept Islam in 1153 CE, ending the islands' Buddhist era and beginning more than eight centuries of Islamic history.

Kendhoo's own telling is intimate rather than royal. Elders say that during a brief stop on the island, Thabreyzgefaanu asked a local coconut-bearer for a young coconut to drink. He found it remarkably sweet, and is said to have declared, "this is so sweet — from now on, I name this island Fani Kendhoo," fani being the Dhivehi word for sweetness. The name stuck for generations, and is still recalled by island elders today.

"This is so sweet — hereafter I name this island Fani Kendhoo."

While he stayed, Thabreyzgefaanu is said to have taught the islanders Islamic ritual and led their daily prayers himself. The spot where he stood to pray was never forgotten — and, in time, it was built over with a small coral-stone house that still bears his name.

🛕 A landmark older than the nation's modern history

The Magaamfulhu

The small house known as the Magaamfulhu — "Thabureyzgefaanuge Magaam" in full — marks the exact place where Thabreyzgefaanu is said to have prayed. Islanders date the structure to over 800 years old, making it one of the more storied heritage sites anywhere in the Maldives.

For centuries it was kept in trust rather than left to chance: the island's chief drew on income from a designated parcel of community land — the goifaraabba — specifically to fund its upkeep. The building seen today dates from a major renovation roughly a century ago, carried out under Sultan Mohamed Shamshudheen III, with Kuda Rikilu Naib Kaleeygefaanu Mohamed Manik recorded as the lead architect.

The Magaamfulhu sits close to the village centre and is treated by islanders as a place of quiet respect — guests are welcome to view it from outside, treating the site and any nearby graves with the same care shown to a place of worship.

🐚 coral stone & coconut timber
🪨 Built from the reef itself

The old Friday Mosque

Kendhoo's historic Friday Mosque is counted among the oldest in Baa Atoll — built, like so much of pre-modern Maldivian architecture, from quarried coral stone with finely carved woodwork inside. These coral-stone mosques are a hallmark of Maldivian craftsmanship: cool, white-walled, and built to outlast the salt air for centuries.

The same sun-bleached coral stone gives many of Kendhoo's older structures, including the Magaamfulhu, their warm, weathered colour — a material drawn directly from the reef the island sits on.

🗓️ A timeline

Eight centuries, one small island

A rough timeline of Kendhoo's recorded story, woven from oral tradition, national history and modern records.

Before 1153

A pre-Islamic Maldives

Like the rest of the archipelago, the islands of Baa Atoll were part of a Buddhist-era Maldives for centuries — seafaring communities who fished, wove coir and thatch, and traded between islands.

1153 CE

Thabreyzgefaanu & the conversion to Islam

The Maldivian court converts to Islam — credited to Abu al-Barakat Yusuf al-Barbari. Kendhoo's tradition holds that he stopped, taught and prayed here, and renamed the island Fani Kendhoo.

Centuries on

The Magaamfulhu is kept in trust

The site of his prayers is marked with a coral-stone house. Income from designated island land is set aside for generations to maintain it, under the island chief's stewardship.

~100 yrs ago

A sultan's renovation

The Magaamfulhu is rebuilt to the form seen today, commissioned under Sultan Mohamed Shamshudheen III and led by architect Kuda Rikilu Naib Kaleeygefaanu Mohamed Manik.

2004

The Indian Ocean tsunami

The 2004 tsunami badly damages Kendhoo's original harbour, along with coastlines across the Maldives.

2011

A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve

Baa Atoll — Kendhoo included — is designated a UNESCO World Biosphere Reserve, recognising the region's coral reefs, mangroves and manta ray aggregations.

2017

A rebuilt harbour

Government works deliver a reconstructed 165 m harbour with a new breakwater and quay wall, replacing the tsunami-damaged original.

Today

A living island

Around 1,000 residents fish, farm small plots, weave thatch, work in nearby resorts, and — for some — still practise the traditional medicine Kendhoo is known for nationwide. The Magaamfulhu still stands, and Fani Kendhoo's coconuts are, by all local accounts, still sweet.
